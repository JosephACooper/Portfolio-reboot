The Portfolio Sprint

The process of designing my portfolio

To me portfolios are an important part of being a designer, they’re needed in order to show off work and ultimately to help get clients and jobs. I didn’t have one, and I needed one. As I’m confident with web development I decided I would sit down one weekend and finally get one up and running.

I also decided that I would document the process of me building the portfolio. There are several reasons for this: to show people how I think when it comes to designing, to analyse my own process, to learn from this and then to ultimately improve as a designer for my next project.

Goals

When I start a project I often write down a list of goals that I want to achieve. Writing down this list helps me identify some key issues I wish to tackle as well as outlining a set of objectives for the finished product.

  1. Mobile first design: mobile traffic is always growing, some recent research showed that for the first time mobile traffic had overtaken desktop/laptop traffic for the first time. This is not the only reason to start with designing for mobile, Google also favours search results for mobile friendly websites and it also drives me to focus on content and user experience due to the small screen sizes and limitations.
  2. Simplicity: the site needs to be simple. Being simple allows the user to gain all the information they need to know without having to spend to long navigating around the site.
  3. Visual: strong use of visuals throughout the site can hopefully help keep people on the site as they add an extra layer of interest. Not only this but strong use of visuals can help people scan through the website quickly in order to extract key pieces of information
  4. Case studies: case studies are a great way of showcasing to people how you think through problems. These are great for numerous reasons, they encourage people to come to your site and see how you work, they’re nice to have for future employees to see and they’re also handy for just reflecting back on what you have done. I want case studies to be one of the main draw points of the site.

Research

As with all projects it is important to research what exists out there. I spent ages browsing portfolios and analysing what I like about certain portfolios and what I think could be improved about them. If you’re looking for a list of some good portfolios check out this article here by my friend Dennis Cortes and also this list here by Brian Lovin.

I was also lucky enough to get the chance to speak to Evan Knight through the Out of Office Hours scheme. I was talking to Evan about creating a portfolio and mentioned a “10 second and 10 minute rule” which your portfolio should pass. The basic idea behind this is that your site should be able to provide someone with all the information they may need or want in 10 seconds but also aim to try and keep the user on the site for up to 10 minutes with in depth case studies and call to actions. This makes a lot of sense as you don’t want potential employees/connections navigating around your site for ages figuring out how to contact you or follow you on social platforms.

Although this is not necessarily as important at this stage I began to research what CSS framework I wanted to use. The reason for this is I wanted to stay away from bootstrap; I knew bootstrap reasonably well and I wanted to push myself to learn something new, I also found that bootstrap sites often look very similar due to all the pre-made components in it, and customising these components was often a pain. After hours of browsing around and playing with multiple frameworks I stumbled across Tachyons by mrmrs, but I will cover this more later.

Design

As stated in my goals I want my website to be built mobile first. So I opened up Sketch and created a series of basic wireframes starting with a mobile sized art-board and working my up towards a Desktop size. Throughout this stage I was keeping the “10 second and 10 minute rule” in mind order to help create a visual hierarchy which was interesting and also a UX which was easy to gather important information about me from. These wireframes would then be the basic skeleton for which my designs would be based on.

Here you can see some quick wireframes looking at potetnial ways of displaying case studies

Here is where things started to get slightly more interesting. I don’t have a developed personal brand yet, so this meant I have no colour palette or existing materials. This is something I wanted to begin fixing. I spent more time than I probably should looking around for colour inspiration and creating combinations until I was happy. After playing around with my two favourite colour ways I decided to get some opinions on which colour scheme people preferred. Unfortunately the opinions were pretty 50/50 which made this choosing which way to go forward slightly more difficult. In the end I actually settled for a very basic palette.

After deciding on the the colour way I experimented with several ways of showcasing work, the options here were either using cards or using a list. I kept experimenting with this throughout the development process and in the end I settled on what you can currently see on the home page - which I guess is kind of a hybrid approach. When the homepage was created I jumped into Atom and started coding everything up. At this point my design process changes slightly as I’m constantly jumping back and forward between code and design and even going back to basic research. For example developing the case study pages meant researching what platforms like Medium do in regards to line length and type size, applying design based on the research and then coding it up. As much as designers love the idea of having some kind of rigorous design process although I find it is often not feasible as you often end up jumping between the various stages.

Code

I’ll start of by saying I am no expert at front-end development but I do have confident knowledge of HTML and CSS.

As I mentioned earlier I wanted to build my site on top of Tachyons. Tachyons is a CSS framework but it has a different approach to your typical frameworks, the way it works is kind of similar to inline styling (this may sound hideous but trust me take a look at the site and give it a go). On top of this Tachyons is mobile first and also built on top of an excellent typographic and scaling system. With Tachyons I was able to build this whole site with less 40 lines of additional CSS. Tachyons is definitely something I will be using in future web projects.

Another tool I used for the first time was BrowserSync, this is also something I will definitely be using in my workflow in the future. In case you didn’t know BrowserSync live reloads your webpages every time you make a change, saving me pressing “cmd + R” every-time I want to preview the site in browser. It also allowed me to preview my site extremely easily on mobiles and tablets so I didn’t have to play around with Inspect element.

After the majority of the website was developed I decided to add in some final touches through the use of animations. I wanted these anitmations to be quick and not get in the way too much. Some of these aniations were custom made and some have come from animate.css

Conclusion

Overall I am very happy with the result considering the whole process took just under one week. There are a few things I would like to change. I already have new ideas for a redesign and also after hearing several things about react and then watching Dennis Cortes (I really recommend you follow him) build his site using it I definitely want to look to using next time.

Although I am not completely happy with the outcome — for the time being the sites serves its purpose well and meets the criteria I set out to achieve.

TL;DR

I designed and developed the website you’re currently looking at over the space of a week in order to get my name and work out there. The website was built with the help of CSS framework Tachyons.
